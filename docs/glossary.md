# Glossary

Key terms used throughout this repository, defined in plain language.

Terms are listed alphabetically. Technical depth is kept minimal here — expanded explanations live in the relevant topic files.

---

## A

**Attack Surface** — The sum of all points where an attacker could attempt to enter or extract data from a system. A smaller attack surface means fewer opportunities for exploitation.

**Authentication** — The process of verifying that a user, system, or piece of data is what it claims to be.

**Authorization** — The process of determining what an authenticated user or system is permitted to do.

**APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)** — A sophisticated, usually state-sponsored threat actor that maintains prolonged, stealthy access to a target network. The term describes capability and intent, not just a single attack.

---

## B

**Backdoor** — A covert method of bypassing normal authentication to gain unauthorized access to a system. Can be intentionally placed by an attacker or inadvertently introduced by developers.

**Botnet** — A network of compromised computers (bots) controlled remotely by an attacker, typically used for distributed attacks, spam, or cryptocurrency mining.

**Brute Force** — An attack method that systematically tries all possible combinations of passwords or keys until the correct one is found.

---

## C

**C2 (Command and Control)** — Infrastructure used by an attacker to communicate with and issue commands to compromised systems.

**CVE (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures)** — A public catalog of known security vulnerabilities, each assigned a unique identifier (e.g., CVE-2021-44228 for Log4Shell).

**CVSS (Common Vulnerability Scoring System)** — A standardized framework for rating the severity of security vulnerabilities on a scale of 0–10.

**Credential Stuffing** — An attack that uses stolen username/password pairs from one breach to attempt login on other services, exploiting password reuse.

---

## D

**Data Breach** — An incident in which sensitive, protected, or confidential information is accessed, disclosed, or stolen without authorization.

**Defense in Depth** — A security strategy that layers multiple independent defenses so that if one fails, others remain.

**DMARC / DKIM / SPF** — Email authentication protocols that help prevent email spoofing and phishing.

---

## E

**Enumeration** — The process of actively gathering information about a target system — users, services, shares, and other details — typically after initial access.

**Exploit** — Code or a technique that takes advantage of a vulnerability to cause unintended behavior in a system.

**Exfiltration** — The unauthorized transfer of data from a system or network to an attacker-controlled location.

---

## F

**Firewall** — A network security device or software that monitors and filters incoming and outgoing traffic based on defined rules.

**Forensics (Digital)** — The process of collecting, preserving, and analyzing digital evidence, typically following a security incident.

---

## H

**Hash** — A fixed-length output produced by a hash function from input data. Used for integrity verification and password storage. A good hash function is one-way and collision-resistant.

**Honeypot** — A decoy system designed to attract and detect attackers while gathering intelligence about their methods.

---

## I

**IOC (Indicator of Compromise)** — Observable evidence that a system may have been breached — unusual file hashes, suspicious IP addresses, unexpected registry changes.

**IR (Incident Response)** — The organized process for detecting, containing, eradicating, and recovering from a security incident.

---

## L

**Lateral Movement** — Techniques an attacker uses to progressively move through a network after gaining initial access, seeking higher privileges or valuable targets.

**Least Privilege** — The security principle that users and systems should have only the minimum access rights necessary to perform their function.

---

## M

**Malware** — Software designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorized access to systems. Includes viruses, worms, ransomware, spyware, and trojans.

**MFA (Multi-Factor Authentication)** — Authentication that requires two or more verification factors — something you know, something you have, or something you are.

**MITRE ATT&CK** — A publicly available knowledge base of adversary tactics and techniques based on real-world observations. Used for threat modeling, detection, and response.

---

## P

**Patch** — A software update that fixes a vulnerability or bug.

**Payload** — The part of malware or an exploit that performs the intended malicious action.

**Persistence** — The methods an attacker uses to maintain access to a compromised system across reboots or credential changes.

**Phishing** — A social engineering attack delivered via email (or other communication channels) that deceives victims into revealing credentials, clicking malicious links, or downloading malware.

**Privilege Escalation** — Gaining higher privileges than originally assigned, either on a local system (local privilege escalation) or across the network (vertical privilege escalation).

---

## R

**Ransomware** — Malware that encrypts a victim's data and demands payment for the decryption key.

**Reconnaissance** — The information-gathering phase of an attack, where an attacker maps the target before attempting exploitation.

**Red Team** — A group that simulates adversary attacks against an organization to test defenses. Contrasts with Blue Team (defenders).

---

## S

**SIEM (Security Information and Event Management)** — A system that aggregates and analyzes security event data from across an environment to detect threats and support investigations.

**Social Engineering** — Psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or revealing information, rather than exploiting technical vulnerabilities.

**SOC (Security Operations Center)** — A team responsible for monitoring, detecting, investigating, and responding to cybersecurity events.

**Supply Chain Attack** — An attack that targets a less-secure element in the supply chain — a software vendor, hardware manufacturer, or service provider — to compromise downstream customers.

---

## T

**Threat Actor** — Any individual, group, or nation-state that poses a cybersecurity threat. Categorized by capability, intent, and target.

**Threat Modeling** — A structured process for identifying and prioritizing potential threats to a system.

**TTP (Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures)** — The behaviors and methods that characterize how a threat actor operates. Used to attribute and detect attacks.

---

## V

**Vulnerability** — A weakness in a system, application, or process that could be exploited by a threat actor.

**VPN (Virtual Private Network)** — A technology that creates an encrypted tunnel between a device and a network, masking traffic from network observers. Does not make a user anonymous.

---

## Z

**Zero-Day** — A vulnerability that is unknown to the vendor and has no available patch. Exploits targeting zero-days are particularly dangerous because no official defense exists yet.

---

*Terms are added as they appear in content across the repository.*
